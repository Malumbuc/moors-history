711 – 1492 CE

The Rise & Golden Age of the Moors

The Moors — a collective name for the Muslim Berber and Arab peoples of North Africa — crossed the Strait of Gibraltar in 711 CE under the command of General Tariq ibn Ziyad, fundamentally changing the course of European history. Within seven years they had conquered nearly the entire Iberian Peninsula, establishing a civilization they called Al-Andalus.

At its height, Moorish Spain was the most advanced society in the Western world. While much of Europe languished in the Dark Ages, the Moors preserved and expanded the classical knowledge of Greece and Rome, pioneering breakthroughs in medicine, mathematics, astronomy, philosophy, and agriculture. Cities like Córdoba, Seville, and Granada were gleaming metropolises with running water, paved streets, libraries of thousands of volumes, and populations in the hundreds of thousands.

"In Córdoba alone there were over 70 libraries, 900 public baths, and 300 mosques — the light of civilization burned brightest where the Moors ruled."

— Medieval historian accounts of Al-Andalus
711 CE
The Conquest of Iberia

Tariq ibn Ziyad crosses from Morocco with 7,000 Berber troops, lands at the great rock that would bear his name — Jabal Tariq (Gibraltar) — and defeats the Visigothic King Roderic at the Battle of Guadalete. The conquest of the peninsula follows swiftly.

756 CE
The Emirate of Córdoba

Umayyad prince Abd ar-Rahman I escapes the massacre of his dynasty in Damascus, reaches Spain, and establishes the independent Emirate of Córdoba. He begins construction of the great Mezquita — the Mosque-Cathedral of Córdoba — one of the wonders of the medieval world.

912 – 961 CE
The Caliphate — Golden Age

Under Abd ar-Rahman III, Al-Andalus reaches its peak. He declares the Caliphate of Córdoba, the most powerful state in Europe. Scholars, poets, physicians, and scientists flood his court. Jewish, Christian, and Muslim scholars collaborate freely — a period called convivencia, or coexistence.

1238 CE
The Sultanate of Granada

As the Reconquista slowly reclaims the north, the Nasrid dynasty establishes the last great Moorish kingdom: Granada. They build the Alhambra Palace — a masterpiece of Islamic architecture and to this day one of the most visited sites in the world.

January 2, 1492
The Fall of Granada

Sultan Muhammad XII — known as Boabdil — surrenders the keys of Granada to Queen Isabella I and King Ferdinand II, ending 781 years of Moorish rule in Spain. According to legend, as he departed he wept at the sight of the Alhambra. His mother said: "You weep like a woman for what you could not defend as a man." The era of Al-Andalus was over.

Achievements That Changed the World

Medicine & Science

Ibn Rushd (Averroes) and Ibn Sina's medical texts were used across Europe for centuries. Moorish physicians performed advanced surgeries, described the circulatory system, and established the first hospitals.

Mathematics

The Moors brought Arabic numerals — including the concept of zero — to Europe, replacing the unwieldy Roman numeral system. Algebra itself takes its name from the Arabic al-jabr.

Architecture

The Alhambra, the Mezquita of Córdoba, and the Giralda Tower of Seville stand as enduring monuments to Moorish genius — their arches, geometric tilework, and muqarnas ceilings have never been surpassed.

Agriculture

The Moors introduced irrigation systems, crop rotation, and dozens of new foods to Europe: oranges, lemons, figs, pomegranates, cotton, sugar cane, and rice — transforming the Spanish landscape forever.

Primary Source

Antonio de Sosa & His Moorish Chronicles

Who Was Antonio de Sosa?

António de Sosa (c. 1545–1587) was a Portuguese Augustinian friar and scholar whose extraordinary captivity in Algiers produced some of the most vivid and detailed accounts of Moorish civilization ever written. Captured by Moorish corsairs in 1577, he spent five years as a slave in Algiers — the same prison that held Miguel de Cervantes, the author of Don Quixote.

Rather than despairing, de Sosa transformed his captivity into scholarship. He wrote compulsively — observing, interviewing, and documenting the Moorish society around him with the eye of an anthropologist centuries ahead of his time.

The Portrait of a Noble Moor

De Sosa's masterwork, the Topographia e Historia General de Argel (written c. 1580, published 1612), contains detailed portraits of the Moorish leaders and people he encountered. Chief among these is his portrait of Hassan Pasha (Uluj Ali) — the Beylerbey (Governor-General) of Algiers — whom de Sosa depicts as a man of fierce intelligence, commanding physical presence, and complex moral character.

De Sosa writes of Hassan Pasha with grudging admiration: a former Christian slave who rose to become the most powerful man in North Africa, ruling with a combination of cunning diplomacy and martial strength. His Moor is neither the cartoonish villain of medieval Christian literature nor a romanticized noble savage — he is a fully realized human being of towering ambition and real contradictions.

"He was tall, of olive complexion, with the bearing of a king... his mind moved faster than any man I have known, Christian or Moor, and his memory was a treasury of all knowledge."

— António de Sosa describing Hassan Pasha, Topographia e Historia General de Argel, c. 1580

Why de Sosa Matters

At a time when European literature routinely dehumanized Moorish people, de Sosa's chronicles insist on their full humanity. His work is a primary source that scholars today use to understand daily life, power, culture, and the complex cross-cultural encounters of 16th-century Mediterranean civilization. His Moors are not props or enemies — they are the central subjects of a vanished world he was determined to preserve.

De Sosa's writings were also personal: he documented the suffering of Christian slaves, including Cervantes, but he was equally unflinching in recording the dignity, humor, learning, and nobility of the Moorish people who held them.

Then & Now

The Moore Family — A Living Legacy

The surname Moore (and its variants: Moor, More, Maure) carries within it a direct echo of the Moorish presence in Europe. Derived from the Latin Maurus — meaning "dark-skinned" or "from Mauretania" — it was applied throughout medieval Europe to describe people of North African or Moorish descent. When the Moors were expelled from Spain after 1492, many converted to Christianity (becoming Moriscos) and took or were given surnames that encoded their origins.

Today, millions of people across the world bear the Moore surname, and a growing movement of scholars, genealogists, and cultural historians are tracing its deep roots back to Al-Andalus and North Africa. For many African Americans in particular, the Moore name carries a layered history — tied both to the legacy of enslavement and, further back, to the noble Moorish civilizations that preceded the transatlantic slave trade.

The Morisco Diaspora
1492 – 1614 CE

After the fall of Granada, hundreds of thousands of Moors who converted to Christianity — the Moriscos — remained in Spain for over a century. Many took the surname Moore (or Moro/Maure) as a marker of identity. When they were finally expelled in 1609–1614, their descendants scattered across North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and, through colonial pathways, eventually the Americas.

Andre Moore
Contemporary Legacy Bearer

Among the many notable figures who carry the Moore name today, Andre Moore represents a generation that has sought to understand and honor the full depth of their name's heritage. Scholars, athletes, artists, and community leaders named Moore across the African diaspora are increasingly researching the Moorish roots of their surnames — reconnecting with a civilization their ancestors helped build.

The Moorish Science Movement
20th – 21st Century

Founded by Noble Drew Ali in 1913, the Moorish Science Temple of America was one of the first organized movements to assert Moorish identity among African Americans — claiming descent from the ancient Moabites and Moors of North Africa. This movement seeded decades of cultural pride and historical inquiry into Moorish ancestry across Black America.

Reclaiming the Heritage
Identity & Scholarship

Today historians, DNA genealogists, and cultural organizations across the United States, United Kingdom, and Europe are working to document Moorish lineages. The Moore family name — in all its variants — connects millions of living people to one of history's most remarkable civilizations. That connection is not merely symbolic: it is genetic, cultural, and spiritual.

"We were not always slaves. Before the chains, there were palaces. Before the palaces, there was the desert. And before the desert — there was a civilization that lit the world."

— Contemporary Moorish heritage scholar
